We asked over 1000 people from all walks of life in 21 countries about how they viewed teachers in society
and discovered how differently teachers are viewed across the world.

R-E-S-P-E-C-T

China affords teachers higher status than any other country.
The next highest? Greece, Turkey, Singapore and New Zealand.
The lowest? Israel, Brazil, Czech Republic and Italy.

In the majority of countries surveyed (for example, New Zealand, Spain and the
Netherlands), teachers are considered similar to social workers in terms of their profession.
In China they are considered most like doctors, in France and Turkey, they are considered
to be most like nurses, and in the USA, most like librarians.

In China, 50% of those surveyed would definitely encourage
their children to become teachers. In Israel, only 8% would.

TEACH YOUR
CHILDREN WELL

Status isn’t always linked to just salaries. In Greece the status of
teachers is high, but their compensation is low. On the other hand,
in Germany and Switzerland, even though teacher earnings
are relatively high, respect is low.

ARE TEACHERS
PAID ENOUGH?

In South Korea, France
and the US, teachers are
paid significantly more
than people think they get
paid or think they deserve.

An average of 75% of
people across 21 countries
agree that teachers should
be paid according to
student performance.

There is a clear and positive relationship between teacher relative pay and performance. In most
countries, people think teachers should be paid more. In South Korea, France and the US, teachers
are paid significantly more than people think they do get paid or should get paid.

An average of 75% of people across all countries agree that teachers should be paid according to
student performance.

In Japan, Greece and the US, many people think unions have too much influence over
teacher pay & conditions. But in Finland, Switzerland and Singapore, they don’t think
unions have enough power.

In countries with higher educational outcomes, people are significantly less likely to
think that teachers’ unions hold too much influence.
